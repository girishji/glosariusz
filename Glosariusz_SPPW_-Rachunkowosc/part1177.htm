Another staff role in larger firms is as a specialized knowledge worker to help mainstream staff with complex technical matters. As well as financial results, there are other non-financial performance criteria that most account- ing firms will strive to achieve:

◆ Client satisfaction;

◆ Quality and motivation of staff and partners;

◆ Staff and partner training;

◆ Fostering a pro-active “can do” culture;

◆ Ongoing marketing, business development and a prospect “pipeline”;

◆ Billing that reflects the value to the client rather than the time input;

◆ Investment in new service offerings;

◆ Remuneration structures that reflect actual contribution to the practice.

Like most professions with limited entry and high performance standards, accountants are richly rewarded. Just how richly rewarded depends on their ability to run their prac- tices in a business-like manner that maximises owner value. For further reading, see David Meister, “Managing the Professional Services Firm”.

Behind accounting principles stands an even more abstract conceptual framework. Accounting principles are broad which leaves room for individual interpretation. As well, there are inherent trade-offs amongst them (e.g. matching and conservatism). Accordingly, different countries – each with their own traditions, legal system and industrial structure – will invoke distinctive accounting standards as specific laws.
