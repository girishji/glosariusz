Start-up Companies, Bank Financing of • Start-upy, finansowanie bankowe (potoczne) – As stated in the excerpted quote for small business lending, “obtaining a loan, especially for start-ups, is next to impossible.” Com-

mercial bankers are indeed inherently reluctant to finance what is, in effect, a “market experiment” (see quote below). Launching a brand new business (“start-ups”) is very risky by nature. Future revenues, expenses and investment levels are all unquantifiable. So too is management capacity. In Canada, for ex- ample, about 70% of first time business ventures are typically closed down or bankrupt within five years. In the absence of a track record, it is impossible to predict in advance whether a new, untried business will survive, let alone pros- per. (This is why the franchising model is so viable: once a business model has proved itself, it is easier to replicate it elsewhere.) To add to a commercial bank’s reluctance to help start a business are its low lending margins (inability to share upside) and the presence of risk-takers moral hazard (when gambling with other people’s money). That said, many a new business has been launched with some help from bank credit. Typically, this comes from a branch office having suf- ficient confidence in the idea to support it by exercising discretionary lending authority. This is where local, community knowledge has a real advantage over mass financial products that are designed by a head office. If such bankers de- cided to support a business start-up, they would likely do it on a judicious basis:

Reliable Sponsor

◆ Legitimate (commercial vs. bored or passionate) reasons to start a new venture

◆ A track record of relevant skills and honouring of personal debt obligations

◆ A vested stake in the business; all personal wealth committed upfront

Business Plan

◆ An explicit written plan with transparent and realistic assumptions

◆ A downside contingency plan that shows resilience to disappointing results

◆ A commercially attractive business model that can survive healthy scepticism

Second Way Out

◆ Collateral security that is independent of the firm’s fortunes

◆ Recourse to the sponsor/manager to cover any shortfalls in debt service

◆ Close monitoring against budget plans; quick response to any deviations


So commercial banks are capable of financing start-ups but only on fairly strict conditional terms. This is why so many start-ups end up relying on internal sources of finance such as the so-called “Three Fs” (i.e. “family, friends and fools”). What of Schumpeter’s notion of creative destruction (i.e. the backing of entrepreneurial innovators who shake up the established system and promote

dynamic growth)? When Schumpeter spoke of the role of “financiers”, he would have been thinking of business angels, venture capitalists, merchant bankers or private equity funds, all sources of capital with a risk appetite that matches a start-up. Thus, the money is out there for start-ups. Entrepreneurs just need to go “shopping” in the right place. Once a new business has some track re- cord under its belt (substantiated by financial statements) then most banks will treat them as more bankable propositions. An exception to commercial banks avoiding start-ups is in project financing where turnkey projects may often be financed. However, such financing engages large and credible sponsors and will be carefully structured (e.g. completion guarantees, off-take agreements etc.) to address a project’s risks. The other exception for start-ups is in those cases where government guarantee schemes exist that are designed to promote en- terprise formation as part of a broader economic development policy. In these situations, banks are really acting as distribution channels for a government programme as opposed to taking start-up risks themselves.
